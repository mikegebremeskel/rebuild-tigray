# STYLE.md — Rebuild Tigray

This document governs how we write: the words we choose, how we punctuate, how we handle names and terminology, and what we expect from contributors. It exists so that everything on this site reads as though it came from a single considered voice, whether that's a staff post, a contributed interview, or an org profile written by someone else.

When in doubt, ask: is this clear? Is it honest? Does it respect the subject?

---

## Voice and tone

Rebuild Tigray is not an advocacy organization. We do not campaign, petition, or lobby. We document, amplify, and connect. That distinction should show in the writing.

The voice is direct and unhurried. We do not editorialize where facts are enough. We do not soften where clarity serves better. We do not perform urgency — the work speaks for itself.

Think of it less like a nonprofit website and more like a literary journal that happens to cover diaspora. We write for people who are paying attention, not people who need to be convinced to care. That means we trust the reader, keep our own voice out of the way of the subject, and let the people we spotlight carry the weight.

**Do:** Write the way a thoughtful person speaks. Use the first person when it is yours to use. Let a sentence breathe.

**Do not:** Use nonprofit boilerplate ("empower," "uplift," "foster," "leverage," "drive impact"). Do not write in the passive voice to avoid taking a position. Do not moralize.

---

## Sentence case everywhere

All headings, subheadings, navigation labels, button text, and page titles use sentence case. Capitalize the first word and proper nouns only.

**Correct:** No mountain we would not climb  
**Incorrect:** No Mountain We Would Not Climb

The only exception is "REBUILD TIGRAY" as a wordmark — that is always all caps when used as the logo lockup, and never otherwise.

## Punctuation

**Oxford comma:** Always use it. The ambiguity it prevents is not worth the aesthetic of dropping it.

Correct: health, education, and advocacy  
Incorrect: health, education and advocacy

**Em dashes:** Do not use them. If you need to set off a clause, use a comma pair or rewrite the sentence. A sentence that requires an em dash usually needs restructuring.

**En dashes:** Use for ranges (2020–2022, pages 14–18). No spaces on either side.

**Ellipses:** Acceptable in direct quotes where something is omitted. Not for stylistic trailing off.

**Exclamation marks:** Do not use them. Emphasis is earned through word choice, not punctuation.

**Colons:** Use to introduce a list or a clause that directly explains what precedes it. Do not capitalize the word that follows a colon unless it is a proper noun or begins a full independent sentence.

**Quotation marks:** Double quotes for speech and direct quotation. Single quotes for a quote within a quote. Periods and commas go inside closing quotation marks.

---

## Numbers

Spell out numbers one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.

Spell out any number that begins a sentence, or rewrite to avoid it.

Use numerals for: percentages (8%), years (2020), ages (a 34-year-old doctor), and money ($500).

Ranges: use an en dash with no spaces (2020–2022, $200–$500).

---

## Dates

Write dates in full: November 4, 2020. Not 11/4/20, not 4 November 2020.

When the year is clear from context, month and day is sufficient: November 4.

---

## Capitalization

**Tigray:** Always capitalized. Never "tigray."

**Tigrayan:** Always capitalized when referring to the people or culture.

**Tegaru:** Capitalized. This is the preferred term for the people of Tigray and Tigrinya-speaking communities when used in that specific cultural context. "Tigrayan" is also acceptable and more familiar to a general audience. Do not use them interchangeably within the same piece — pick one and stay with it.

**Diaspora:** Lowercase unless it begins a sentence or is part of a formal proper noun. "The Tigrayan diaspora" not "the Tigrayan Diaspora."

**The conflict / the war / the genocide:** We use "genocide" when referring to the 2020–2022 war on Tigray. This is the accurate term, affirmed by scholars and investigators. We do not hedge it with quotation marks or qualifiers. When more specificity is needed: "the 2020 war," "the Tigray war," or "the conflict in Tigray."

**Acronyms:** Always spell out the full term on first use, followed by the acronym in parentheses. Use the acronym alone on subsequent references. This applies to organizations, agreements, bodies, and any other abbreviated term — no exceptions, even for acronyms that feel universally known. If an acronym is not used again after its introduction, do not introduce it.

Example: The Cessation of Hostilities Agreement (CoHA) was signed in November 2022. The CoHA has since been only partially implemented.

Do not introduce an acronym and then never use it. If you only reference something once, just use the full name.

**Organizations:** Use the full proper name on first reference. Abbreviations are acceptable on subsequent references if the organization is well-known within the diaspora context (e.g., Omna Tigray → Omna, SJT). Spell out on first use in every article.

**Government and political bodies:** Capitalize formal names (Ethiopian National Defense Forces, Tigray People's Liberation Front). Do not capitalize informal or descriptive references (federal forces, the regional government).

## Tigrinya and Ge'ez

Tigrinya and Ge'ez words and phrases appear in their original script when context allows it, followed by a transliteration in parentheses on first use, then an English translation or explanation.

Example: ዘይንድይቦ ጎቦ ዘይንሰግሮ ሩባ (zeynidiyibo gobo zeynisegiro ruba) — there are no mountains we would not climb, no rivers we would not cross.

Do not italicize Tigrinya or Ge'ez text in running prose. Italics for foreign-language text is a convention inherited from a tradition that treated non-English as foreign. These words are not foreign to this site or its subjects.

On second reference, the original script or transliteration alone is fine.

If a contributor provides a transliteration but not the original script, use what is provided. Do not invent or guess.

## Interviews and contributed writing

**Interviews:** We publish edited conversations, not verbatim transcripts. Our role is to make the subject's voice as clear as possible, not to preserve every filler word. However, we do not put words in people's mouths or smooth out accents, cadence, or phrasing that is distinctly theirs. If a subject speaks in Tigrinya and the interview is translated, note this at the top.

**Voice preservation:** Do not edit someone's English into a different register. If a subject writes or speaks directly and without polish, leave it direct and without polish. If they use long sentences, let them use long sentences. Your job is clarity, not uniformity.

**Attribution:** Every blog post and contributed piece carries a byline. No anonymous posts, except in rare documented cases where safety is a genuine concern — and even then, note that the name has been withheld for safety.

**Reasonable edits:** Contributors agree to edits for clarity, fact accuracy, and style compliance. They review the final version before publication. We do not change the substance of what someone has said.

**Org profiles:** Written collaboratively with the organization where possible. The organization reviews for accuracy before publication. We write the profile — they do not write it themselves — but we fact-check against what they tell us.

---

## What we do not publish

Content that names or implies a target for harm. Unverified claims presented as fact. Political endorsements of candidates or parties. Content that singles out individuals within the diaspora for criticism by name, except where those individuals are public figures acting in their public capacity. Anything written primarily to settle a score.

We are not apolitical — the situation in Tigray is political and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. But we do not publish content whose primary purpose is partisan positioning rather than genuine documentation or analysis.

## A note on tone for sensitive content

Much of what we cover involves atrocity, loss, and displacement. We do not sensationalize. We do not use suffering as atmosphere. When a piece requires describing violence or trauma, describe it plainly and move on. The reader does not need the weight amplified by the prose — the facts carry it.

At the same time, we do not sanitize. The word "genocide" is in this guide for a reason. Clarity is a form of respect.
