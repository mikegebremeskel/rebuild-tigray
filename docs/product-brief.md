# Product Brief — Rebuild Tigray

*A one-page product brief. The "what" and "why" before the "how." Last updated 2026-06.*

---

## The problem

The work of rebuilding Tigray is already happening — but it's invisible and fragmented.

Dozens of diaspora organizations across continents are doing real work: advocacy, humanitarian relief, cultural preservation, professional networking. Most of it is volunteer-run, unpaid, thankless, and **scattered**. No single place shows who is doing what, in their own words.

This fragmentation has a cost:

- **Diaspora members who want to help, reconnect, or find community can't easily find who's doing the work** — especially people who've moved cities or continents, and a second generation at risk of losing the language and identity entirely.
- **The organizations doing the work lack visibility and support**, so they burn out, duplicate effort, and struggle to recruit. Energy that should go toward the work goes toward division instead.
- **The diaspora stays divided** — by money, power, politics, and city — when the one thing everyone agrees on is wanting a safe, thriving Tigray.

Other diasporas (Armenian, Korean) built connective infrastructure *in peacetime*, so it was ready when crisis came. Tigray is trying to build it in the middle of the fire. Post-Pretoria, with the headlines fading, the window to build that infrastructure is **now** — before urgency disappears and volunteers drift away.

## Who it's for

Two users, one platform.

**1. The diaspora member who cares.**
A Tigrayan in the diaspora who wants to contribute, reconnect, or simply not feel alone in caring — including the second generation growing up far from the homeland.
> *Job to be done:* "Help me find people and organizations I can trust and join, so I can contribute and stay connected to who I am."

**2. The organization doing the work.**
A small, often volunteer-run diaspora org — under-resourced and frequently invisible to anyone outside its immediate circle.
> *Job to be done:* "Let us be seen accurately, in our own words, so the right people find us, join us, or support us."

## The strategy (the bet)

> **Visibility creates empathy. Empathy creates common ground. Common ground creates collaboration.**

Rebuild Tigray is a **lens, not a megaphone**. It is explicitly *not* a news outlet, a political organization, or a fundraising platform. It shows the work and the people doing it — unblemished, no spin, no agenda — so others find them, learn from them, join them, or feel less alone. The bet is that honest visibility, at scale, pulls a divided diaspora toward the common ground it already shares.

## Scope

**In scope (v1):**
- The manifesto — the "why," stated plainly up front
- A curated directory of organizations, each in their own words
- Stories and interviews with the people doing the work
- A gallery of what's being fought for — the land, the people, the future

**Explicitly out of scope (and why):**
| Deferred | Why |
|----------|-----|
| Open user-submitted org listings | Trust and accuracy at launch matter more than coverage. Curate first, open up later. |
| Accounts / auth | No user-data need yet; it adds surface area and risk for zero current benefit. |
| Donations / fundraising | Taking money compromises the "no agenda" neutrality the whole bet rests on. |
| Connection / matching features | High value but high complexity. Earn the audience first, then build the network layer. |
| Tigrinya / multilingual | Worth doing *right*, not fast. English-first reaches the broadest diaspora at launch. |

## Success metrics

Honest framing: this is **early-stage**. The right metrics now are *leading indicators* that prove the engine works — not vanity numbers.

**Leading (what I watch now):**
- **Directory coverage** — # of verified organizations listed (is the lens worth looking through?)
- **Discovery** — did a visitor reach an org detail page? (did they *find* someone?)
- **Intent to connect** — outbound clicks to org websites
- **Inbound pull** — organizations asking to be listed (the clearest signal of value)

**Lagging (what I'd track at scale):**
- Weekly active contributors / new volunteers who report connecting *via* the site
- Engagement among second-generation diaspora (the culture-loss risk this addresses)

**Current state (honest):** in a deliberate **discovery phase** — a Typeform is live gathering feedback to shape the next version, and product analytics are intentionally *not* instrumented yet (the right instrument at this stage is qualitative feedback, not a dashboard). See the [measurement plan](measurement-plan.md) for what we measure, and ignore, at each stage. I'd rather report an honest zero than inflate a vanity metric.

## Key risks

- **Neutrality is the product.** Given how divided the diaspora is (TPLF vs. interim government, secession vs. federalism, city rivalries), the moment this site is seen as taking a side, the bet collapses. Every editorial choice protects perceived neutrality.
- **Accuracy & consent.** Representing real orgs and people carries a duty to get it right. (See the decision to withhold interviewee names pending consent in the [decision log](decisions.md).)
- **Sustainability.** Solo-curated. The model has to stay low-maintenance or it won't survive — which directly shaped the file-based, PR-driven architecture.
- **Sensitivity.** This is an active conflict. Featuring individuals can expose them; safety considerations gate what gets published.
